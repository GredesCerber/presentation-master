Slide 3 ~1.5 min
What is Artificial Intelligence?

So what exactly is AI? Marvin Minsky, one of the founding fathers of AI at MIT, defined it as "the science of making machines do things that would require intelligence if done by humans."

At its core, AI systems have three key capabilities: Learning — the ability to acquire knowledge from data; Reasoning — drawing logical conclusions; and Problem Solving — finding optimal solutions to complex challenges.

Unlike traditional software that follows explicit rules, AI can learn patterns and make decisions based on experience.

Key Points

  • AI mimics human cognitive functions
  • Three pillars: learning, reasoning, problem-solving
  • Differs from traditional rule-based programming
Slide 4 ~1.5 min
History of AI

AI has a fascinating history spanning over 70 years. In 1950, Alan Turing proposed his famous test for machine intelligence — can a machine fool a human into thinking it's human?

The term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference. The field had its ups and downs, including "AI winters" when funding dried up.

A major milestone came in 1997 when IBM's Deep Blue defeated chess world champion Garry Kasparov. The real breakthrough came in 2012 when deep learning revolutionized image recognition.

Since 2022, we've entered the era of generative AI with systems like ChatGPT, Claude, and DALL-E.

Slide 5 ~1.5 min
Types of Artificial Intelligence

AI is typically categorized into three types based on capability levels.

Narrow AI, or ANI, is what we have today. It excels at specific tasks — like voice assistants, image recognition, or recommendation systems — but can't transfer skills to other domains.

General AI, or AGI, would match human-level intelligence across all cognitive tasks. This is what researchers are working toward but haven't achieved yet.

Super AI, or ASI, would surpass human intelligence entirely. This remains theoretical and is the subject of much speculation about AI's long-term future.

Slide 7 ~1.5 min
Machine Learning Approaches

Machine learning is the engine that powers modern AI. There are two main approaches.

Supervised learning uses labeled training data — we show the system examples with correct answers, and it learns to predict answers for new data. This is used for classification and regression tasks.

Unsupervised learning works with unlabeled data, discovering hidden patterns on its own. It's used for clustering, like grouping customers by behavior, and for detecting anomalies.

There's also reinforcement learning, where AI learns through trial and error, receiving rewards or penalties — like how AlphaGo learned to play Go.

Real-World Applications

AI is transforming virtually every industry. In healthcare, AI assists with disease diagnosis, accelerates drug discovery, creates personalized treatment plans, and analyzes medical images with superhuman accuracy.

In transportation, we're seeing autonomous vehicles, smart traffic management, optimized route planning, and predictive maintenance that prevents breakdowns.

In finance, AI powers fraud detection systems that protect billions in transactions, algorithmic trading, risk assessment, and customer service chatbots.

These are just a few examples — AI is also revolutionizing education, agriculture, manufacturing, and entertainment.

Slide 9 ~1.5 min
How Neural Networks Work

Neural networks are inspired by the human brain. They consist of layers of interconnected nodes or "neurons."

The input layer receives raw data. Hidden layers — and deep learning uses many of these — process and transform the information. The output layer produces the final result.

Training follows a cycle: forward pass to make a prediction, calculate the error, backward pass to determine how to adjust, update the connection weights, and repeat millions of times until the network becomes accurate.

This is how systems learn to recognize faces, translate languages, or generate text.

Slide 10 ~1.5 min
Deep Learning Revolution

Deep learning has driven the recent AI explosion. Let me highlight four key architectures.

CNNs — Convolutional Neural Networks — excel at visual tasks. They power face detection, medical imaging, and self-driving car vision systems.

RNNs — Recurrent Neural Networks — handle sequential data like text and speech, enabling translation and voice assistants.

Transformers are the architecture behind modern language models like GPT and Claude. They revolutionized natural language processing.

GANs — Generative Adversarial Networks — create realistic content by having two networks compete with each other.

Slide 11 ~1.5 min
Ethical Considerations

With great power comes great responsibility. AI raises significant ethical challenges.

Algorithmic bias can perpetuate discrimination if training data reflects historical biases. Job displacement concerns are real, though often overstated. Privacy risks emerge as AI enables unprecedented surveillance. Deepfakes threaten truth and trust. And many AI systems are "black boxes" — we don't know exactly how they make decisions.

Solutions include diverse training data, reskilling programs, data protection laws, detection technologies, and developing explainable AI that can justify its decisions.

Slide 12 ~1 min
The Future of AI

What does the future hold? In the next 5 years, expect even more advanced language models, mainstream autonomous vehicles, and AI-powered drug discovery saving lives.

By 2030-2040, we may see general-purpose robots, direct brain-computer interfaces, and AI systems conducting independent scientific research.

Beyond 2040, some predict the emergence of AGI and profound human-AI collaboration to solve our greatest challenges.

As Andrew Ng said, "AI is the new electricity" — it will transform every industry just as electricity did a century ago.
