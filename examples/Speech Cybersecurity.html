Slide 3 ~1 min
What is Cybersecurity?

The NIST framework defines cybersecurity as protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information.

It rests on three pillars: Confidentiality — keeping data from unauthorized access. Integrity — ensuring data hasn't been tampered with. And Availability — keeping systems running when needed.

Think of it like a bank vault — you need to keep thieves out, ensure nothing inside gets altered, and make sure legitimate customers can access their valuables.

Slide 4 ~1.5 min
Cyber Threat Landscape

Let's talk numbers. The global cost of cybercrime hit 8 trillion dollars in 2023. That's larger than most countries' GDP.

There are about 2,200 cyber attacks happening every single day. And here's the scary part — on average, it takes 277 days to detect a breach. That's 9 months of attackers having access to your systems.

Perhaps most importantly, 95% of breaches involve human error. Technology alone can't save us — we need awareness and training.

Looking at threat levels: ransomware is critical, phishing remains extremely high, and data breaches are a persistent concern.

Slide 5 ~1 min
Common Threat Types

Threats fall into three main categories. First, malware — malicious software like viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware. These can destroy data, spy on you, or hold your files hostage.

Second, social engineering — manipulating humans rather than systems. This includes phishing emails, pretexting, baiting, and tailgating into secure areas.

Third, network attacks — DDoS attacks that overwhelm servers, man-in-the-middle attacks that intercept communications, SQL injection, and zero-day exploits that target unknown vulnerabilities.

Slide 6 ~1.5 min
Cyber Attack Lifecycle

Understanding how attacks unfold helps us defend against them. There are seven stages.

Reconnaissance — gathering information about the target. Weaponization — creating the attack payload. Delivery — getting it to the target, often via email.

Exploitation — triggering the vulnerability. Installation — establishing a persistent foothold. Command and Control — setting up remote access. And finally, Actions — stealing data, encrypting files, or whatever the attacker's goal is.

The good news? We can interrupt this chain at multiple points.

Slide 7 ~1 min
Defense in Depth

Effective security requires layers — what we call "defense in depth."

Technical controls include firewalls, intrusion detection systems, encryption, multi-factor authentication, and endpoint protection.

But technology alone isn't enough. Administrative controls are equally important — security policies, employee training, incident response plans, regular audits, and proper access management.

Think of it like a castle — you don't just have one wall, you have moats, walls, guards, and protocols.

Slide 8 ~1 min
Personal Security Best Practices

What can you do today? Four key practices.

Strong passwords — use unique, complex passwords for each account. Better yet, use a password manager.

Enable MFA — multi-factor authentication adds a second layer even if your password is compromised.

Update regularly — patches fix known vulnerabilities. Don't delay them.

Be skeptical — if an email seems suspicious, verify before clicking. It's okay to be paranoid.

Slide 9 ~1.5 min
Incident Response Process

When — not if — an incident occurs, you need a plan. Six phases.

Preparation — have the plan, train the team, set up tools before anything happens.

Detection — monitor systems, analyze alerts, identify indicators of compromise.

Containment — isolate affected systems to stop the spread, but preserve evidence.

Eradication — remove the threat, patch the vulnerabilities that allowed it.

Recovery — restore systems, verify they're clean, return to normal operations.

And critically, Lessons Learned — document what happened and improve your defenses.

Slide 10 ~1 min
Emerging Trends

Where is cybersecurity heading? Four major trends.

AI-powered security — using machine learning for automated threat detection, behavioral analytics, and predictive security.

Zero Trust Architecture — the principle of "never trust, always verify." No implicit trust based on network location.

Quantum-safe cryptography — preparing for the day quantum computers can break current encryption.

Cloud security — as we move to the cloud, SASE architecture and DevSecOps integration become critical.

Slide 11 ~1 min
Key Takeaways

Let me leave you with the key points. Security is everyone's responsibility, not just the IT department's. Defense in depth is essential — no single solution is enough. The human factor is often the weakest link. And preparation beats reaction every time.

Your action items: audit your passwords today, enable MFA everywhere possible, stay informed about new threats, and report anything suspicious.
